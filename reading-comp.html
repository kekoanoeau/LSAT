Reading Comprehension

One scored RC section with 4 passages (~27 questions). One passage is a comparative reading set (two shorter passages). The key is active, structural reading — not re-reading.

The RC Method: Active Passage Mapping

  1. Read for structure, not details. Your goal is to understand the author's argument, not memorize every fact.
  2. Mark the main point of each paragraph in 3–5 words in the margin (mental or written).
  3. Track viewpoints. Identify the author's view vs. views the author is discussing or critiquing.
  4. Note tone words — "however," "unfortunately," "surprisingly," "mistakenly" reveal the author's stance.
  5. Identify the main point before answering questions.
  6. Return to the passage for detail questions — never answer from memory alone.

Passage Subject Areas

Law / Legal Theory

Appellate rulings, legal philosophy, constitutional history, jurisprudence. Common and often dense. Focus on what the ruling held and why.

Natural Science

Biology, physics, geology, ecology. Usually descriptive with a research finding or theory debate. Note what is known vs. hypothesized.

Social Science

Economics, psychology, sociology, anthropology. Often presents a theory and an opposing view. Track who believes what.

Humanities

History, literary criticism, art, philosophy. Strongly opinion-driven — the author has a clear point of view. Author's tone is critical here.

Comparative Reading

Two shorter passages on related topics. Questions ask about relationship, agreement, disagreement, or structure between the two passages.

Reading Strategies

The Passage Map Method

As you read, jot a 3–5 word label for each paragraph's function. After the passage, state the main point in one sentence before reading any question.

  • P1: Background / Setup — introduces the topic and context
  • P2: Complication / New View — introduces the author's argument or a problem
  • P3: Evidence / Support — data, examples, or reasoning backing the main view
  • P4: Conclusion / Implication — wraps up, sometimes addresses objections

Tracking Viewpoints

Many RC passages have multiple views: the author's, scientists', critics', traditionalists', etc. Tag each viewpoint as you read using margin notation:

  • A: Author's own view
  • V1/V2: Views the author is describing
  • C: Critic or opposing view
  • Note when the author agrees or disagrees with a presented view

Time Management (35 minutes / 4 passages)

  • Aim for ~8 minutes per passage (3–4 min reading, 4–5 min questions)
  • Comparative reading often has fewer questions — may go faster
  • If a passage is very difficult, spend less time on details and answer global questions first
  • Never spend more than 90 seconds on a single question — skip and return

RC Question Type Reference

Question TypeStem ClueStrategy
Main Point / Primary Purpose "The main point of the passage is..." / "The primary purpose of the passage is..." Must encompass the ENTIRE passage. Eliminate answers that are too narrow (single paragraph) or too broad. Match tone.
Author's Attitude / Tone "The author's attitude toward X can best be described as..." Find tone words in the passage. Extreme answers (contempt, enthusiastic praise) are usually wrong. Match the degree of positivity/negativity.
Detail / Specific Information "According to the passage..." / "The passage states that..." Return to the passage — never answer from memory. The answer must be directly stated or paraphrased, not inferred.
Inference "The passage most strongly suggests..." / "It can be inferred from the passage..." The answer must be proven by the passage but not explicitly stated. One logical step beyond what's written. Eliminate answers that need outside knowledge.
Organization / Structure "Which best describes the organization of the passage?" Use your passage map. The answer will describe the passage at the level of paragraphs. Match the logical flow.
Function of Paragraph / Phrase "The second paragraph serves primarily to..." Ask: "Why did the author include this?" — to provide evidence, introduce a counterargument, illustrate, qualify, etc.
Analogy / Parallel Structure "Which situation is most analogous to the one described in lines X–Y?" Abstract the key relationship in the passage. Find the answer with the same relationship, not just the same topic.
Comparative (Passage A vs. B) "Both passages agree that..." / "Unlike Passage A, Passage B..." Apply the Commitment Test: both passages must have taken a clear position on the topic for it to count as agreement/disagreement.
Strengthen / Weaken the Argument "Which, if true, would most weaken the argument in lines X–Y?" Treat like an LR Weaken question — find the assumption and attack it. These are rare in RC.

Common Wrong Answer Patterns

Wrong Answer TypeDescription
Out of Scope Introduces information or concepts not mentioned in the passage. Very common trap on inference questions.
Too Extreme Uses absolute language (always, never, all, only) when the passage was more moderate. Check for hedging language in passage.
Opposite / Reversal States the exact opposite of what the passage says. Particularly common on tone and author-agrees questions.
Too Narrow For main point questions, describes only a detail or one paragraph rather than the whole passage.
Distortion Takes a real element from the passage but misrepresents it — twists the author's meaning or overstates a claim.
True but Irrelevant Accurate according to the passage but doesn't answer the question asked. Common trap that lures test-takers.

How to Improve RC

Daily Active Reading

Read dense nonfiction daily — The Economist, law review summaries, scientific journals. Practice mapping structure while reading.

Blind Review

After finishing a timed RC section, go back with unlimited time and mark every question you're not 100% certain about. This reveals systematic errors.

Low-Res Summary Drill

After reading each paragraph, write a 5-word summary. After the full passage, write a one-sentence main point. Verify against the correct "Main Point" answer.

Wrong Answer Analysis

For every wrong answer, categorize WHY it was wrong (out of scope, too extreme, etc.). Tracking patterns reveals your weaknesses.
