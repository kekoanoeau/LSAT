Logical Reasoning

LR now makes up ~65% of your LSAT score. Two scored sections of 24–26 questions each (~50 total LR questions). This is the section where your score is won or lost.

The LSAT Demon Way · lsatdemon.com
"Slow down to speed up. This is The Way."
Nathan Fox (179/180) and Ben Olson's LSAT Demon platform argues the LSAT is fundamentally easy — if you stop using gimmicks and start thinking clearly. No diagramming, no memorized attack patterns, no negation test. Just genuine understanding, one question at a time.

The LR Process (Every Question, Every Time)

  1. Read the stimulus slowly. Never skip a word. Find the conclusion and premises before you reach the question stem.
  2. Attack the argument. Accept the premises as true — but poke holes in the conclusion. Where's the gap? What's the flaw?
  3. Read the question stem to confirm what's being asked (and which category: Passage-Driven or Answer-Driven — see below).
  4. Predict before you look. Cover the answer choices. What would the right answer look like? Form your own answer first.
  5. Eliminate aggressively — all four wrong answers are wrong for specific reasons. Find those reasons.
  6. Pick the best answer. Boring, conservative, vague answers are usually safer than specific or strongly-worded ones.

The Two Categories of LR Questions — LSAT Demon Framework

Every LR question falls into one of two categories. Knowing which you're in tells you how to treat the answer choices.

Passage-Driven · "Closed Record"

Treat the passage as the complete universe of facts. The correct answer is already supported by what's written — no new information enters.

Question types: Conclusion, Must Be True / Supported, Necessary Assumption, Flaw, Role, Method of Reasoning, Parallel, Parallel Flaw, Point at Issue / Disagree
Answer-Driven · "Open Record"

Each answer choice is a new piece of information you treat as true. Ask which new fact best helps (or hurts) the argument.

Question types: Strengthen, Weaken, Sufficient Assumption, Evaluate, Paradox / Resolve
The Flaw Family: Flaw, Necessary Assumption, Strengthen, and Weaken are all interconnected — they all require you to attack the argument and identify its logical vulnerability first. Master flaw identification and these four question types become much easier.

Anatomy of an LSAT Argument

Conclusion

The main point the author is trying to prove. Look for: therefore, thus, hence, so, clearly, it follows that, this shows that.

Demon tip: The conclusion may be a full sentence or just part of one. Always know what the author is trying to prove, not just what they say.

Premise

Evidence used to support the conclusion. Look for: because, since, given that, for, as, in light of, the fact that.

Demon tip: Accept all premises as true. Your job is never to question the facts — only the conclusion drawn from them.

Intermediate Conclusion

A claim that is supported by premises and also supports the main conclusion. Acts as a bridge — it's both a conclusion (proven) and a premise (used to prove more).

Spot it: can you say "So… because…" around it?

Assumption / Gap

The unstated link between premises and conclusion — what the argument silently depends on. The gap is where the argument is vulnerable.

Demon tip: Attack the argument here. The gap is the target for Flaw, Weaken, Strengthen, and Assumption questions.

Question Type Reference

Assumption ~12% of LR

Find the unstated premise the argument depends on to work.

Common Stems

  • "The argument assumes which of the following?"
  • "Which of the following is an assumption required by the argument?"
Strategy: Attack the Argument Identify the gap between premises and conclusion. The correct answer is the one the argument cannot function without — it's the weakest link. LSAT Demon explicitly discourages the Negation Test: understand the gap directly rather than relying on a mechanical shortcut.
Strengthen ~10% of LR

Find the answer that most supports the argument's conclusion.

Common Stems

  • "Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?"
  • "Which most supports the argument above?"
Strategy: Attack First, Then Match Answer-Driven (Open Record): treat each choice as a new true fact. Attack the argument first — find the vulnerability. Then find the answer that directly addresses and supports that gap. New information is not only allowed, it's the point.
Weaken ~12% of LR

Find the answer that most undermines the argument's conclusion.

Common Stems

  • "Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument?"
  • "Which most undermines the conclusion above?"
Strategy: Attack First, Then Undermine Answer-Driven (Open Record): treat each choice as a new true fact. Attack the argument first — identify its vulnerable assumption. Then find the answer that hits that exact vulnerability: an alternative cause, a counter-example, or a fact that severs the premise-conclusion link.
Flaw ~13% of LR

Identify the logical error in the argument's reasoning.

Common Stems

  • "The argument is flawed because it..."
  • "The reasoning is most vulnerable to which criticism?"
Strategy: Name the Gap Passage-Driven (Closed Record): after reading the argument, describe the flaw in your own plain English before reading the choices. "The author assumes correlation is causation." "The author ignores other explanations." Then match. Common flaws: causal, sampling, false dichotomy, scope shift, part-to-whole, circular reasoning, ad hominem.
Inference / Must Be True ~11% of LR

Find what must be true (or is most strongly supported) based only on the stimulus.

Common Stems

  • "Which of the following can be properly inferred?"
  • "Which is most strongly supported by the information above?"
Strategy: Stay in the Box Passage-Driven (Closed Record): 100% proven by the passage, either restated or derived by combining two or more facts. No outside information allowed. The Demon principle: boring, conservatively-stated, vague answers are usually safer than specific, loaded ones — they're easier to prove.
Main Conclusion ~6% of LR

Identify the author's central claim that everything else supports.

Common Stems

  • "The main conclusion of the argument is that..."
  • "Which best expresses the main point of the argument?"
Strategy: Why Test For each answer, ask: "Does the argument give reasons FOR this?" If yes, it could be the conclusion. The conclusion is never supported by another answer choice.
Parallel Reasoning ~6% of LR

Find the answer with argument structure most similar to the stimulus.

Common Stems

  • "Which is most similar in its reasoning to the argument above?"
  • "The flawed reasoning above is most similar to which?"
Strategy: Abstract the Structure Convert the argument to abstract form (e.g., "All A are B; X is A; therefore X is B"). Match the logical form, not the topic.
Principle (Apply) ~8% of LR

Apply a stated principle to a specific situation, or find a principle that justifies the argument.

Common Stems

  • "Which principle, if valid, most helps to justify the reasoning?"
  • "The cases above most closely conform to which principle?"
Strategy: Treat as Strengthen "Justify the reasoning" questions work like assumption/strengthen. Find the rule that bridges the gap between the premises and conclusion.
Point at Issue / Agree ~5% of LR

In a dialogue, find what the two speakers actually disagree (or agree) about.

Common Stems

  • "The dialogue above provides most support for the claim that A and B disagree about..."
Strategy: Commitment Test The correct answer must be something both speakers have taken a clear position on — one agreeing, one disagreeing (or both agreeing). Eliminate any answer where one speaker is silent.
Role of Statement ~4% of LR

Identify what function a boldfaced or specified portion plays in the argument.

Common Stems

  • "The portion in boldface plays which role in the argument?"
  • "The claim that [X] functions as..."
Strategy: Label First Before reading the answers, label each boldfaced portion: main conclusion, sub-conclusion, premise, counter-premise, background, or objection. Match to answer choices.
Sufficient Assumption ~5% of LR

Find the assumption that, if true, guarantees the conclusion follows.

Common Stems

  • "The conclusion follows logically if which is assumed?"
  • "Which, if assumed, allows the conclusion to be properly drawn?"
Strategy: Bridge the Gap Completely Answer-Driven (Open Record): the correct answer must guarantee the conclusion follows — not just make it likely. Find the precise logical bridge. LSAT Demon: understand the connection in plain English rather than through arrow notation. These are highly predictable once you see the gap clearly.
Explain / Resolve ~4% of LR

Find the answer that best explains an apparent discrepancy or paradox.

Common Stems

  • "Which of the following, if true, most helps to explain the discrepancy?"
  • "Which most helps to resolve the apparent paradox?"
Strategy: Embrace Both Facts The correct answer must make BOTH surprising facts true simultaneously. Answers that only explain one side are wrong.

Common Logical Flaws Reference

Flaw Name Description How to Spot It
Causal Flaw Concludes A causes B merely because A correlates with B. Look for correlation → causation jumps. Correct by: alternative cause, reversed causation, coincidence.
Sampling / Hasty Generalization Draws a broad conclusion from an unrepresentative or small sample. "Studies show..." then applies to everyone. The sample may be biased or too small.
False Dichotomy Presents only two options as if no others exist. "Either X or Y; not X; therefore Y." But what about Z?
Ad Hominem Attacks the person making a claim rather than the claim itself. "X says P, but X has a conflict of interest" — doesn't mean P is false.
Circular Reasoning Uses the conclusion as a premise (begging the question). The "support" for the conclusion just restates the conclusion in different words.
Equivocation Uses the same word in two different senses in the same argument. The word "right" meaning moral vs. correct; "law" meaning legal vs. scientific.
Part-to-Whole / Composition What's true of a part is assumed true of the whole. "Each player is excellent, so the team is excellent."
Whole-to-Part / Division What's true of the whole is assumed true of each part. "The company is profitable, so every department is profitable."
Scope Shift Conclusion uses different terms than the premises establish. Premises about "some" conclude about "all"; premises about possibility conclude about actuality.
Appeal to Authority Accepts a claim solely on the basis of who said it. "Expert X says Y is true" — but the expert may be outside their expertise or biased.

Conditional Logic Quick Reference

LSAT Demon's take: The word "contrapositive" never appears on the LSAT. These relationships are just commonsense rephrasing — understand them in plain English. "If it's brick, it can be earthquake-damaged" simply means "if it can't be earthquake-damaged, it isn't brick." No arrow diagrams required.
FormPlain EnglishValid Flip (Contrapositive)
If A → B All A are B / Whenever A, then B If not-B, then not-A
A only if B You can't have A without B If not-B, then not-A
All A are B Every single A is also B If not-B, then not-A
No A are B A and B are mutually exclusive If B, then not-A
Unless B, not A B is required for A to happen If not-B, then not-A
A unless B If B doesn't happen, A will If not-A, then B

Invalid moves: Affirming the consequent (A→B; B; ∴ A — WRONG) and Denying the antecedent (A→B; not-A; ∴ not-B — WRONG). The LSAT loves to bait you into these.
